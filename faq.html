Frequently Asked Questions

Evidence-based answers about PAP, PNF, and EMS — drawn directly from peer-reviewed research.

Post-Activation Potentiation (PAP)

Yes. Heavy pre-activation exercises — such as a loaded back squat before a sprint or jump — can increase muscular power output in the activity that follows. This is the core principle of PAP: using a demanding conditioning exercise to prime the neuromuscular system for subsequent explosive effort. (Wilson et al., 2013)
PAP increases neural activation of the muscles. Following a heavy conditioning exercise, the nervous system remains in a heightened state of excitation, meaning more motor units can be recruited more rapidly during a subsequent explosive movement. This increased neural drive is the primary driver of the potentiation effect. (Blazevich and Babault, 2019)
The optimal rest period for PAP is typically between 4 and 12 minutes. Too short and fatigue from the conditioning exercise masks the potentiation benefit. Too long and the potentiation fades. The sweet spot within this range varies depending on training status and the exercises used. (Kilduff et al., 2008; Chen et al., 2023)
PAP performance effects depend on the balance between fatigue and potentiation — and that balance shifts depending on training status, exercise intensity, and rest period length. In less trained individuals, the fatigue component tends to outweigh the potentiation benefit, which is why PAP has less effect on untrained athletes than on well-trained ones. (Tillin and Bishop, 2012; Chiu et al.; Wilson et al., 2013)
PAP protocols typically use high-intensity strength exercises at 80% of 1 repetition maximum (1RM) or above. Examples include heavy squats, deadlifts, or bench press performed at low repetitions before the target explosive movement (e.g. a vertical jump, sprint, or throw). The high load is what generates the neuromuscular potentiation. (Seitz and Haff, 2015)

Proprioceptive Neuromuscular Facilitation (PNF)

PNF stretching usually involves contracting the target muscle before stretching it further. In a typical sequence, you move a limb to the end of its range, contract the muscle isometrically (against resistance), then relax and move deeper into the stretch. This contract–relax cycle exploits the nervous system's own inhibitory mechanisms to allow a greater range of motion.
Yes — one PNF stretching session can significantly increase joint range of motion temporarily. However, this acute increase is largely neurological and does not mean the muscle has permanently lengthened. Repeated sessions over time produce more lasting flexibility adaptations. (Konrad and Tilp, 2014)
Two mechanisms work together. First, PNF stretching works partly because Golgi tendon organs — sensory receptors in the tendons — detect tension and reduce muscle activation, allowing the muscle to relax further into the stretch. Second, flexibility improvements are also partly due to increased tolerance to the sensation of stretching, meaning the nervous system becomes less resistant to reaching a greater range of motion. (Hindle et al., 2012; Magnusson et al., 1996)
Caution is warranted. PNF stretching performed immediately before exercise may temporarily reduce strength or power performance. This is consistent with broader evidence on static and PNF stretching in warm-ups. PNF is better placed at the end of a session or on dedicated flexibility days when strength and power performance during the session matters. (Behm et al., 2015)
No. PNF stretching can be performed with both maximal and submaximal muscle contractions, and a number of PNF techniques — such as contract–relax using a strap or your own hands for resistance — can be performed independently. A partner can make certain positions easier to hold, but is not required for an effective session.
PNF is widely used in both sports training and physiotherapy to improve flexibility and mobility. Its origins lie in neurological rehabilitation, and it remains a staple technique used by physiotherapists, strength and conditioning coaches, and sports scientists across many disciplines. (Hindle et al., 2012)

Electrical Muscle Stimulation (EMS)

EMS devices send electrical pulses through electrodes placed on the skin. These external electrical impulses trigger muscle contractions by depolarising the motor neurons beneath the electrodes — the same nerve signals that cause muscles to contract voluntarily, but generated externally rather than by the brain. (Milosevic et al., 2020)
EMS mainly stimulates the muscles directly under the electrode pads. The electrical current does not spread uniformly through the body — stimulation is localised to the tissue between and beneath the electrodes. This is why electrode placement is important for targeting specific muscles.
Yes. EMS training can increase muscle strength, and evidence shows it can improve strength and power performance in athletes when used as a supplement to conventional training. However, it is not a direct replacement for voluntary resistance training, which also develops coordination, motor patterns, and joint stability that EMS alone cannot replicate. (Maffiuletti and Maffiuletti, 2010; Filipovic et al., 2012)
During voluntary exercise the body normally recruits slow-twitch fibres before fast-twitch ones (the size principle). EMS bypasses this order — it can recruit fast-twitch muscle fibres that are difficult to activate during voluntary exercise, and can activate large motor units earlier than voluntary contractions would. This non-selective recruitment is one reason EMS can produce training stimuli that differ from conventional exercise. (Gregory and Bickel, 2005; Yoo et al., 2023)
EMS is often used in rehabilitation to help maintain muscle when someone cannot exercise normally — for example after surgery, injury, or during prolonged immobilisation. By producing muscle contractions passively, EMS can slow the rate of muscle atrophy and maintain some neural activation of the muscle when voluntary movement is limited or impossible.
