The statements below come from the Myth Library and the Asteroid Blaster game. Each one is matched to a peer-reviewed paper or authoritative guideline. Tap View citation on any row to reveal the reference.

Myth

Stretching before exercise prevents injury

Lauersen JB, Bertelsen DM, Andersen LB. "The effectiveness of exercise interventions to prevent sports injuries." Br J Sports Med. 2014;48(11):871–877.
Myth

Muscle turns into fat when you stop training

Staron RS et al. "Muscle hypertrophy and fast fiber type conversions in heavy resistance-trained women." Eur J Appl Physiol. 1990;60(1):71–79. Muscle and adipose tissue are physiologically distinct; one cannot convert to the other.
Myth

Crunches burn belly fat

Vispute SS, Smith JD, LeCheminant JD, Hurley KS. "The effect of abdominal exercise on abdominal fat." J Strength Cond Res. 2011;25(9):2559–2564.
Myth

Cardio is the only way to lose weight

Strasser B, Schobersberger W. "Evidence for resistance training as a treatment therapy in obesity." J Obes. 2011;2011:482564.
Myth

Lifting weights makes women bulky

Staron RS et al. "Strength and skeletal muscle adaptations in heavy-resistance-trained women after detraining and retraining." J Appl Physiol. 1990;70(1):134–143.
Myth

No pain, no gain

American College of Sports Medicine. ACSM's Guidelines for Exercise Testing and Prescription. 10th ed. Lippincott Williams & Wilkins; 2018. Exercise at moderate perceived exertion (RPE 12–14) produces health benefits without pain.
Myth

Sweating means you are burning fat

McArdle WD, Katch FI, Katch VL. Exercise Physiology: Nutrition, Energy, and Human Performance. 8th ed. Lippincott Williams & Wilkins; 2014. Sweating is a thermoregulatory response, not a measure of fat oxidation.
Myth

Running always damages your knees

Lo GH et al. "Is there an association between a history of running and symptomatic knee osteoarthritis?" Arthritis Care Res. 2017;69(2):183–191.
Myth

You need protein shakes to build muscle

Morton RW et al. "A systematic review, meta-analysis and meta-regression of the effect of protein supplementation on resistance training-induced gains in muscle mass and strength." Br J Sports Med. 2018;52(6):376–384.
Myth

More exercise is always better

Kreher JB, Schwartz JB. "Overtraining syndrome: a practical guide." Sports Health. 2012;4(2):128–138.
Myth

You only use 10% of your brain

Chudler EH. "Myths about the brain." Neuroscience for Kids, University of Washington. Neuroimaging studies show virtually all brain regions are active. See also: Boyd R. Scientific American. 7 Feb 2008.
Myth

Eating carbs at night causes weight gain

Sofer S et al. "Greater weight loss and hormonal changes after 6 months diet with carbohydrates eaten mostly at dinner." Obesity. 2011;19(10):2006–2014.
Myth

Lactic acid causes post-workout soreness

Cheung K, Hume P, Maxwell L. "Delayed onset muscle soreness: treatment strategies and performance factors." Sports Med. 2003;33(2):145–164. Lactate clears within an hour of exercise; DOMS is caused by micro-damage and inflammation.
Fact

Exercise improves mental health

Schuch FB et al. "Exercise as a treatment for depression: a meta-analysis adjusting for publication bias." J Psychiatr Res. 2016;77:42–51.
Fact

Sleep is essential for muscle recovery

Dattilo M et al. "Sleep and muscle recovery: endocrinological and molecular basis for a new and promising hypothesis." Med Hypotheses. 2011;77(2):220–226.
Fact

Dehydration reduces exercise performance

Sawka MN et al. "American College of Sports Medicine position stand. Exercise and fluid replacement." Med Sci Sports Exerc. 2007;39(2):377–390.
Fact

Strength training increases bone density

Layne JE, Nelson ME. "The effects of progressive resistance training on bone density: a review." Med Sci Sports Exerc. 1999;31(1):25–30.
Fact

Regular exercise reduces heart disease risk

Warburton DE, Nicol CW, Bredin SS. "Health benefits of physical activity: the evidence." CMAJ. 2006;174(6):801–809.
Fact

Warming up improves performance

Bishop D. "Warm up II: performance changes following active warm up and how to structure the warm up." Sports Med. 2003;33(7):483–498.
Fact

Resistance training boosts metabolism

Westcott WL. "Resistance training is medicine: effects of strength training on health." Curr Sports Med Rep. 2012;11(4):209–216.
Fact

Good posture reduces back pain

Hartvigsen J et al. "What low back pain is and why we need to pay attention." Lancet. 2018;391(10137):2356–2367.
Fact

Progressive overload builds strength

Schoenfeld BJ. "The mechanisms of muscle hypertrophy and their application to resistance training." J Strength Cond Res. 2010;24(10):2857–2872.
