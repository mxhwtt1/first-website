Technique Overview

Electrical Muscle Stimulation

EMS — using electrical impulses to support recovery and rehabilitation.

What is it?

Electrical Muscle Stimulation (EMS) uses mild electrical impulses delivered through electrodes placed on the skin to cause muscles to contract. Your muscles contract without you having to voluntarily move.

In a clinical setting, EMS is used by physiotherapists to maintain or rebuild muscle in people who cannot exercise in conventional ways — for example, following surgery, severe illness, or in the early stages of cardiac rehabilitation when exercise tolerance is very limited.

EMS is different from TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation), which is used to block pain signals rather than to contract muscles.

How does it work?

1

Electrodes are placed on the skin

Adhesive pads are positioned over the target muscle. The placement determines which muscle is stimulated.

2

Electrical pulses mimic nerve signals

The device sends pulses that replicate the signal your motor nerves would normally send to tell the muscle to contract. The muscle cannot tell the difference.

3

The muscle contracts and relaxes

The muscle contracts on each pulse and relaxes between them, just as it would during voluntary exercise. Frequency and intensity are controlled by the therapist.

4

Repeated sessions build strength and circulation

Over time, EMS sessions help maintain muscle mass, improve local blood flow, and support the transition to active exercise.

Key facts

⚡

Clinically supervised

Therapeutic EMS used in cardiac rehab is set and monitored by a physiotherapist — not the same as consumer fitness devices sold in shops.

🚫

Not suitable for everyone

EMS must not be used over the chest in people with pacemakers or implantable defibrillators (ICDs). Always tell your team about any implanted devices.

🩹

Safe when used correctly

Clinical EMS has a strong safety record when used by trained professionals. The sensation is usually described as a tingling or tapping feeling.

📈

Supports but does not replace exercise

EMS is a useful adjunct for those who cannot yet exercise conventionally. As fitness improves, active exercise takes over as the primary training mode.

✅

Preserves muscle during bed rest

After a cardiac event requiring hospital admission, EMS can help prevent the muscle loss that occurs with inactivity and accelerate recovery.

📋

Evidence-based in cardiac rehab

Studies in patients with heart failure and post-cardiac surgery show EMS can improve muscle strength and exercise capacity when conventional exercise is not yet possible.

Myth vs Fact

Fact: EMS can stimulate local muscles but does not provide the cardiovascular, metabolic, and whole-body benefits of active exercise. It is a rehabilitation and adjunct tool, not a shortcut to fitness. Consumer EMS suits marketed as "effortless workouts" are not supported by evidence as exercise replacements.
Fact: EMS applied to leg or arm muscles at correct parameters does not affect heart rhythm in people without implanted devices. However, electrodes must never be placed on the chest or across the heart. Clinicians follow strict placement protocols to ensure this. Always disclose any cardiac implants before any EMS treatment.
Fact: Clinical EMS devices are medical-grade, calibrated, and used by trained professionals who assess your suitability, monitor your response, and adjust parameters in real time. Consumer devices vary widely in quality and safety. If you are considering using one at home, discuss it with your team first.
Medical disclaimer EMS is not appropriate for everyone, particularly those with implanted cardiac devices. Never use an EMS device without first consulting your cardiac rehab team or GP. This page provides general educational information only.
